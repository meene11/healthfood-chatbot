========================================
Iron
Fact Sheet for Consumers
National Institutes of Health | Office of Dietary Supplements
========================================

KEY FUNCTIONS

Iron serves critical roles in the body:
- Helps create hemoglobin for oxygen transport in red blood cells
- Creates myoglobin for muscle oxygen supply
- Supports hormone production
- Supports growth and development

RECOMMENDED INTAKE

Daily requirements vary by age and sex:

Life Stage                    | Recommended Amount
------------------------------|-------------------
Infants birth-6 months        | 0.27 mg
Infants 7-12 months           | 11 mg
Children 1-3 years            | 7 mg
Children 4-8 years            | 10 mg
Children 9-13 years           | 8 mg
Teen boys 14-18 years         | 11 mg
Teen girls 14-18 years        | 15 mg
Adult men 19-50 years         | 8 mg
Adult women 19-50 years       | 18 mg
Adults 51+ years              | 8 mg
Pregnant teens                | 27 mg
Pregnant women                | 27 mg
Breastfeeding teens           | 10 mg
Breastfeeding women           | 9 mg

NOTE: Vegetarians require nearly double these amounts since plant-based iron (nonheme iron) absorbs less efficiently than iron from animal sources (heme iron).

FOOD SOURCES

Iron appears naturally in:
- Lean meats, seafood, poultry (heme iron - more readily absorbed)
- Beans, lentils, spinach, peas (nonheme iron)
- Nuts, dried fruits
- Tofu, fortified cereals and breads

The body absorbs nonheme iron from plants better when consumed alongside animal proteins or vitamin C-rich foods such as citrus fruits and broccoli.

SUPPLEMENTATION FORMS

Iron supplements typically contain:
- Ferrous sulfate
- Ferrous gluconate
- Ferric citrate
- Ferric sulfate

IMPORTANT WARNING: All iron-containing supplements carry warning labels about keeping them away from children, as accidental overdose represents a leading cause of fatal poisoning in young children.

WHO IS AT RISK OF DEFICIENCY?

Higher-risk populations include:
- Adolescent girls and women with heavy menstrual bleeding
- Pregnant women
- Frequent blood donors
- People with gastrointestinal disorders (celiac disease, inflammatory bowel disease)
- People with cancer
- People with heart failure

DEFICIENCY SYMPTOMS

- Weakness and fatigue
- Difficulty concentrating
- Reduced ability to fight infection
- In severe deficiency: iron-deficiency anemia (pale skin, shortness of breath, dizziness, headaches)

HEALTH EFFECTS ON SPECIFIC CONDITIONS

Anemia:
Iron-deficiency anemia is the most common type of anemia worldwide. Supplementation can restore iron levels and improve symptoms.

Pregnancy:
Iron deficiency during pregnancy may contribute to premature birth and low birth weight. Pregnant women need significantly more iron than non-pregnant women.

TOXICITY AND UPPER LIMITS

Upper safe limit for adults: 45 mg/day from all sources

Excessive iron intake causes:
- Gastrointestinal distress
- Constipation and nausea
- Abdominal pain

High doses can harm organ function, particularly the liver.

HEMOCHROMATOSIS: People with this inherited iron-storage disorder must avoid iron supplements. Their bodies absorb too much iron, which can damage the liver, heart, and pancreas.

DRUG INTERACTIONS

Iron reduces the effectiveness of:
- Levodopa (Parkinson's disease medication)
- Levothyroxine (thyroid hormone medication)

Calcium supplements may interfere with iron absorption - take at different times of day.

Certain antibiotics (fluoroquinolones, tetracyclines) should not be taken at the same time as iron supplements.
